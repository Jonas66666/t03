Understanding Energy Ratings

In Australia, the Energy Rating Label helps consumers compare the energy efficiency of appliances. The more stars, the more energy efficient the appliance is compared to other models of similar size.

The Evolution of TV Energy Efficiency

Television technology has come a long way in terms of energy efficiency. Here's how we've progressed:

2000s

Plasma & Early LCD Era

Plasma TVs consumed 300-500W, while early LCDs used 150-250W. Energy efficiency was not a primary concern for manufacturers.

1-2 Star Rating
2010s

LED Revolution

LED backlighting reduced consumption to 80-150W. Energy Star ratings became standard, driving efficiency improvements.

3-4 Star Rating
2020s

Smart Efficiency

OLED and QLED technologies with smart sensors and automatic brightness adjustment. Consumption dropped to 50-100W.

5-6 Star Rating

Compare Within Size

Always compare TVs of similar screen sizes, as larger screens naturally consume more power.

Check Standby Power

Efficient TVs use less than 0.5W in standby mode. This can save $10-20 annually.

Auto-Brightness Feature

TVs with ambient light sensors can reduce brightness in dark rooms, saving 20-30% energy.
